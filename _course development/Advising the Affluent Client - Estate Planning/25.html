The Unified Tax Rate Schedule

Let's now begin a deeper exploration of the federal estate and gift tax system by exploring the Unified Tax Rate Schedule. In so doing, we will also begin looking at the Economic Growth and Tax Relief Reconciliation Act of 2001 (the 2001 Tax Relief Act), the Tax Relief, Unemployment Insurance Reauthorization, and Job Creation Act of 2010 (the 2010 Tax Relief Act), and the American Taxpayer Relief Act of 2012 (The 2012 Tax Relief Act). While it is tempting to simply look at the transfer tax laws as implemented by the 2012 Tax Relief Act, it is important that you also have an understanding of the laws as they previously existed for the following reasons:

 

  1. Many clients have previously done planning, which might be different than the plans that would be put in place today. You need to understand the rationale behind those pre-existing plans before you can assist them in exploring how they might want to revise them.

  2. The 2010 and 2012 Tax Relief Acts are simply revisions of the past, making it quite easy to explain the current rules once you understand what they revised. In other words, rather than restating the tax laws, these Tax Acts presuppose an understanding of prior tax laws, making it difficult to understand them by themselves.

 

The following is the unified tax rate schedule that existed in 2001. This schedule has continued to be the basis for both estate and gift tax calculations in subsequent years, but as we shall see, subsequent years have brought a dramatic decrease in the maximum rate on this schedule.
