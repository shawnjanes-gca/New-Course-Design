Annual Gift Tax Exclusion

Person or institution to whom a gift is made.

In addition to the deduction for transfers to a spouse or charity, which results in no transfer taxation, there are also three types of lifetime gifts that are excluded from consideration for transfer taxes. What is especially important to remember about these three types of excluded gifts is that they not only escape gift taxation when gifted, they also generally are never added back to the estate as post-1976 gifts when we die.

The first of these is known as the annual gift tax exclusion. For 2015, lifetime gifts of a present interest in the amount of$14,000 or less can be made annually (indexed for inflation in future years) to any individual, and are excluded from the gift tax.  Note that the gift tax exclusion is not limited to gifts to family members; it applies to gifts to ANY INDIVIDUAL.

This provides a significant ability for individuals with taxable estates to effectively transfer assets out of their estates without incurring a transfer tax. Given sufficient donees and sufficient years to make transfers, sizeable amounts can effectively avoid taxation.

A husband and wife can “elect to” or “consent to” split gifts between them for a combined $28,000 (in 2015) to any individual, and the entire amount qualifies for the annual gift tax exclusion. This is true even if the source of the funds is from one spouse.

An individual can make $14,000 annual gifts to as many people as desired.

In other words, it is either used or lost. If a year is skipped, the individual is not allowed to carry it forward into a subsequent year.

Beginning in 1999, the exclusion was set at $10,000 and indexed for inflation using 1997 as the base year. The indexing is rounded down to the next lowest multiple of $1,000, meaning it may be a number of years between changes. For 2015, it is $14,000.
