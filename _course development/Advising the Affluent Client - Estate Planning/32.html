Case Study - Joe and Martha Andrews

To better understand the techniques that were utilized to do estate planning prior to the 2010 Tax Relief Act, let’s look at a case study that takes place in 2009, when the rules governing estate and gift taxation were governed by the 2001 Tax Relief Act. This will provide a foundation from which we can better understand the rules and planning techniques appropriate for 2010 and beyond.

The Story of Mr. and Mrs. Andrews (as it existed in 2009)

Joe and Martha Andrews, both U.S. citizens, are a married couple in their early 60's, with three grown children. Their combined taxable estate in 2009 was $3,700,000. Their existing plan at the time was to leave everything to the surviving spouse; then upon the death of the second spouse to leave everything to their children.*

*The characters and scenarios in this case study are fictitious and purely for purposes of illustration.

Each spouse had an applicable exclusion amount of $3,500,000. Combined, that totals $7,000,000, more than enough to cover the $3,700,000 of their combined estate. Yet they ended up owing a sizeable estate tax bill when the second spouse died.

This scenario highlights the fact that the single biggest mistake couples made in their estate plans prior to the 2010 Tax Relief Act was to leave everything to the surviving spouse. While this worked when estates were relatively small, it was not usually optimal once the combined estates were larger than the applicable exclusion amount. In larger estates, as we have just seen, it caused the loss of the applicable credit amount in the estate of the first to die. For Joe and Martha Andrews, this failure to utilize both applicable credit/exclusion amounts results in a tax bill of $90,000. Had their estate been larger, this avoidable bill could have been even higher.
