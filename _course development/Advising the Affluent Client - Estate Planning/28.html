Increase of Applicable Exclusion Amounts

The Tax Relief Acts of 2001, 2010, and 2012 have resulted in significant changes to the size of the Applicable Credit/Exclusion Amounts since their adoption. Also, while there have been periods of time when the full Credit Amount could be applied to lifetime gifts, there have also been times when only a portion of the Credit could be used while alive, resulting in a “Gift Tax Exclusion” amount that was a subset of the Applicable Exclusion Amount.

To get some appreciation for the magnitude of these changes and to gain an understanding of the changes generated by these Tax Relief Acts, including changes yet to come, take a few minutes to review the table below. Be sure to carefully read the commentary on each of the columns as knowledge of these facts will help you significantly in understanding the planning technique discussed on subsequent pages. You should not expect to remember all the numbers, but you should commit to memory the following:

  • The current year's (2015) numbers.

  • The year in which the estate tax was repealed.

Estate Tax Applicable Exclusion Amount

The 2001 Tax Relief Act increased the Applicable Exclusion Amount for estates from $675,000 in 2001 to $3,500,000 in 2009. All assets in the estate were allowed to step-up their tax basis to the date of death value or the value at the alternate valuation date (6 months after death), whichever was used on the estate tax return. For 2010, the estate tax was repealed, but a limit was imposed on the amount by which the tax basis of estate assets could be stepped up in value.

The 2010 Tax Relief Act reinstated the estate tax for 2010. The default position was to grant a $5,000,000 estate tax exclusion and an unrestricted step-up in basis; alternatively, the Act allowed the executor to opt for the arrangement formerly called for under the Tax Relief Act of 2001, whereby for 2010 there was no estate tax but a limitation on the amount by which the tax basis of assets could be stepped up. The choice resulting in the most favorable tax treatment essentially depended upon the size and the tax basis of the estate assets.

The 2010 Tax Relief Act further increased the Applicable Exclusion Amount to $5,000,000 for 2011 and adjusted it for inflation in 2012. Here, too, a full step-up in basis was allowed. 

Then came the 2012 Tax Relief Act. The 2012 Tax Relief Act extended the $5,000,000 (plus an inflation adjustment) Applicable Exclusion Amount to 2013 and beyond ($5,430,000 for 2015). It also raised the maximum tax rate from 35% to 40% for 2013 and beyond. The full step-up in basis was also retained.

Gift Tax Exclusion Amount

Under the Tax Relief Act of 2001, the full Applicable Exclusion Amount was available for lifetime gifts through 2003. For 2004-2009, the portion that could be excluded for lifetime cumulative gifts was capped at $1,000,000 (excluding spousal transfers, charitable gifts, annual exclusion gifts, and payment of tuition or health care). In 2010, when the estate tax was repealed, the Gift Tax was NOT REPEALED; it remained in place with a $1,000,000 lifetime exclusion.

Then came the Tax Relief Act of 2010. As we shall discuss later, under the Tax Relief Act of 2010, the full Applicable Exclusion Amount ($5,000,000 in 2011 and indexed for inflation to $5,120,000 in 2012) again became fully available in 2011 and 2012 for cumulative lifetime gifts. This was extended by the 2012 Tax Relief Act for 2013 and beyond.
