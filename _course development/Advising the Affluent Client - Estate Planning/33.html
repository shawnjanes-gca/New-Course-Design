Preserving the Applicable Credit/Exclusion Amount in the First Estate

In estate planning prior to the 2010 Tax Relief Act, the scenario in the preceding case study led to two rules-of-thumb in avoiding this loss of the applicable credit amount in the first estate.

Divide up the estate if the combined estate is larger than the applicable exclusion amount. 

Each spouse needed to have sufficient assets in their respective estate to take advantage of their applicable credit/exclusion amount. For Joe and Martha, this would have involved splitting their assets equally so that there would be $1,850,000 in each estate.

Don't plan to transfer all assets to the surviving spouse if the combined estate is larger than any potential applicable exclusion amount.

Instead, the strategy was to apply the applicable credit amount in the first estate and prevent those assets against which it is applied from going into the estate of the surviving spouse. This could be done in one of two ways:

  • Name other heirs. The only trouble with this approach was that it denied the surviving spouse the benefit of the assets, which was something that Joe and Martha Andrews wished to avoid.

  • Transfer the assets into a Credit Shelter Trust. This topic is discussed on the next page.
