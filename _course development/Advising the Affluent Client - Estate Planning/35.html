Case Study - George and Wilma Diamond

In Joe and Martha's situation, they could each cover their estate tax exposure by use of the Credit Shelter Trust.  Let's now look at what strategies were employed if the first estate exceeded the amount that could be protected in a Credit Shelter Trust.  

Using the following information, begin identifying the Diamond’s estate needs. Again, for the time being, let’s continue to examine the case study from the perspective of 2009.

The Story of Mr. and Mrs. Diamond

In 2009, George and Wilma Diamond were both in their early 70's.  They had recently celebrated their 50th wedding anniversary. George was in poor health and was concerned that he would predecease Wilma, who had never handled any of the financial affairs, and he feared she would be overwhelmed by having to handle things herself. They had four grown children, all of whom were married, and twelve grandchildren. One son was handicapped, and George and Wilma still provided some supplemental support for this son and his family. Mr. Diamond expressed a desire to somehow give his handicapped son priority treatment over the other children after his and Wilma’s death, making sure that he would be adequately supported during his lifetime.

George and Wilma's combined estate was valued at $7.8 million in 2009. This included a $500,000 life insurance policy with very low cash value that George owns on his own life. Their income was lower than they would have preferred, in part caused by equity holdings that paid little if any dividends.  Those equity holdings had an extremely low basis, and the Diamonds were reluctant to sell them to reinvest for greater income because they did not want to pay the capital gains tax.

Their wills at the time called for everything to be left with the survivor between them.  After both deaths, the wills directed that everything should be distributed equally between the four children.

Step 2 - Utilize the Applicable Credit/Exclusion Amount

The preceding analysis demonstrated a significant estate tax exposure. We know from the previous case study that they should consider doing two things to preserve both applicable credit/exclusion amounts:

  1. Split assets.

  2. Utilize Credit Shelter Trusts in their estate plans.

Let's assume the Diamonds took both these steps, splitting their assets so that each has at least $3,500,000 in his/her own name. For simplicity, let's suppose they split everything equally, giving each of them $3.9 million. Unlike the previous scenario, they are still left with a taxable estate because the ability for each of them to exclude $3,500,000 ($7,000,000 combined) from taxation is simply not sufficient to cover all their assets.

 

Step 3 - Utilize the Estate Tax Deductions

If their plan utilizes the unlimited marital deduction to pass assets that cannot be protected by the applicable credit amount to the survivor, there would be no tax upon the first death.  Thus, upon the first death, $3,500,000 would go into the credit shelter trust and $400,000 would transfer to the surviving spouse's estate.  If this was done, what would be the estimated tax liability upon the second spouse's death?
