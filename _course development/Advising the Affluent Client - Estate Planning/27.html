We have now discussed five transfers that are not subject to gift or estate taxes:

  • Transfers to a spouse
  • Transfers to a qualified charity
  • Annual Exclusion gifts
  • Tuition Payments
  • Health Care Payments

These transfers are never even considered in calculating the tentative estate or gift tax.

Additionally, each individual has a lifetime credit against estate and gift taxes. A “credit” is an amount that you get to subtract after a tax is calculated. So, once we calculate a tentative transfer tax on the remaining balance, we get to subtract the credit amount from the calculated tax.

The amount of this credit has grown considerably since 2001, when it was $220,550. For 2015, this credit amount is $2,117,800.  This credit is “unified,” meaning it is shared with lifetime transfers and transfers at death. Thus, the credit can be used to offset calculated gift or estate taxes; but if it is all used up by lifetime gifts, there will be no credit left to protect assets that transfer at death.

This credit is typically referred to as the "applicable credit amount." It is also referred to as the "unified credit."

In 2001, the applicable credit amount “excluded” $675,000 of transferred assets from taxation that would have otherwise been taxed. The tax on a $5,430,000 taxable transfer would be equal to the 2015 Applicable Credit Amount of $2,117,800.  Therefore, the 2015 Applicable Credit Amount of $2,117,800 will effectively "exclude" $5,430,000 from estate and gift taxation.  This excluded amount is known as the "Applicable Exclusion Amount.”
