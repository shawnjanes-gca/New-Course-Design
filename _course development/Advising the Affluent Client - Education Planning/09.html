Coverdell Education Savings Accounts (ESAs)

The Coverdell Education Savings Account (ESA) is an education savings vehicle that is in many ways similar to a Traditional IRA, allowing individuals to set aside up to $2,000 per year tax deferred in an account earmarked for education.

Advantages and Disadvantages of ESAs

Tax-Deferred Investment Earnings

Income and capital appreciation generated by the investments in a Coverdell ESA are tax-deferred while in the plan and tax-free when used for qualifying educational expenses. As a result, the assets in the account will grow more than if invested in a comparable taxable account. However, nonqualified distributions will incur income tax at ordinary income tax rates; Capital gains rates are not available.

Tax-Free Distributions for Qualified Education Expenses

Qualified distributions from a Coverdell ESA are not subject to income tax.  Qualified distributions include distributions made to pay educational expenses for:

  • Primary education
  • Secondary education
  • Higher education

If there is a balance in the Coverdell ESA at the time the beneficiary reaches age 30, it must be distributed within 30 days.

Self-Directed Investments

Just as in an IRA, Coverdell ESAs allow individuals to self-direct the investments in the account.

Ability to Change Beneficiaries

Generally it is possible to change beneficiaries as long as, at the transfer date, the new beneficiary is under age 30 and a member of the same family.

  

Limits on Participation

In 2015, eligibility is phased out for single filers with Adjusted Gross Income (AGI) of $95,000 - $110,000 or, for Joint Filers, $190,000 - $220,000.

These limits mean that many high-income earners cannot capitalize on the tax advantages of this type of plan.

Contribution Limits

Another disadvantage is the relatively low annual contribution limit of $2,000 per child per year. For many, $2,000 per year is simply not enough to adequately fund the enormous future costs of a college education as described earlier in this course.

Required Distributions at Age 30

If there is a balance in the Coverdell ESA at the time the beneficiary reaches age 30, generally it must be distributed to the beneficiary within 30 days. Income tax and a penalty will result.

However, instead of taking a taxable distribution, it may be possible to either change the beneficiary of the existing account (see “Advantages” above) or roll over the remaining funds into another Coverdell ESA for the new beneficiary on an income tax-free basis.

Tax-free rollovers require the following:

  • New beneficiary must be under age 30

  • New beneficiary must be part of the same family as the old beneficiary

  • The rollover must be completed within 60 days of the distribution

Special Note about Coverdell Plans

Tax-free withdrawals from Coverdell ESA plans do not qualify for the American Opportunity Tax Credit or the Lifetime Learning Credit.
