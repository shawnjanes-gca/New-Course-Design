Term Life Insurance

Term life insurance has no cash value and offers no savings or investment benefit. But it does provide a death benefit if death occurs during a stated period of time (typically a year). That makes it the most basic and least expensive type of life insurance available.Â  Term life insurance is usually issued in one of the forms listed below. Click each form to learn more.

Renewable Term

Can be renewed at the end of the term, typically with no requirement for evidence of insurability, but there is usually a limit to the number of times. While this provides ongoing coverage, it typically comes at the cost of increasing premium rates with each renewal.

Convertible Term

Allows the policy owner the right to convert to a permanent policy, without requiring evidence of insurability, although this right makes the insurance more expensive. The conversion is usually limited in some way, either stating the age by which it must be converted or limiting the percentage of the term coverage that can be converted. Premiums upon conversion may be computed in one of two ways:

  • Attained Age Conversion - Rate is based on the insured's age at the time of conversion.
  • Original Age Conversion - Rate is based on the insured's age when the term policy was originally purchased. Of course, this would result in lower premiums than the Attained Age Conversion.

Level Term

This indicates that the death benefit remains constant throughout the term, and is usually used in policies with terms that span multiple years. It is also typical for the premiums to remain unchanged during the term.

Decreasing Term

Usually used in policies with terms that span multiple years, providing a death benefit that declines during the course of the term, although premiums usually remain the same.

Increasing Term

This, too, is usually used in policies that have terms spanning multiple years and indicates there is an increasing death benefit during the term (usually accompanied with increasing premiums).

Inexpensive Short-Term

The initial premiums of term life insurance are generally less than those of permanent life insurance. That makes it more affordable for younger clients with limited funds. It also makes it the better choice when the need for life insurance is for a short, limited time, as might be the case when required to have term insurance before receiving approval on a loan.

Expensive Long-Term

But over time, term insurance premiums typically increase as the policy is renewed for the same amount of coverage. Permanent insurance is issued with a level premium that is higher than term insurance when issued at a given age, but becomes less expensive than term insurance as the insured ages.

Thus, the appeal of low initial premiums must be balanced against the fact that premiums of term life insurance will be higher in later years. Where the need for life insurance is long-term, total cost of insurance may be less with permanent insurance. It is also important to keep in mind that one's insurability might decline over the years, and coverage might eventually be lost with term insurance when it could have been retained with permanent insurance.
