How Much of a Traditional IRA Contribution is Deductible?

Contributions to a Traditional IRA are generally, but not always, deductible on an individual’s income tax return. The tax deductibility of those contributions is phased out if the individual meets two conditions:

1. The individual is an active participant in an employer-sponsored retirement plan

AND

2. The individual’s modified Adjusted Gross Income (MAGI) exceeds specified limits. Click here for more information on Modified AGI.

Many of your clients will participate in an employer-sponsored retirement plan. You need to understand when they can and cannot deduct contributions to a Traditional IRA. Why? If they cannot deduct their Traditional IRA contribution, a Roth IRA may be a better financial choice! For those reasons, the following detailed review is presented.

Employer-Sponsored Retirement Plan

This category includes all of the qualified plans - SEP IRAs, SIMPLE IRAs, and 403(b) Plans. We will analyze these plans subsequently in this course.

Modified AGI

Recall that the front page of a 1040 tax return is used for calculation of Adjusted Gross Income (AGI). The back page is used to take deductions against (AGI) to derive taxable income and to calculate the tax. Thus, AGI is the final line on the first page of Form 1040.  For most people, modified AGI is simply AGI before taking the IRA deduction on Line 32. Click here to see Form 1040.

Single - $61,000-$71,000 (2015)

As an example, a single, 34 year-old client is an active participant in an employer-sponsored plan. If, in 2015, this person makes a $2,000 contribution to their IRA, then:

  • With modified AGI of $45,000, the $2,000 contribution would be fully deductible.

  • With modified AGI of $67,000, the $2,000 contribution would generate an $800 deduction calculated as follows.

    To the extent a client’s modified AGI encroaches into the phase-out range, a corresponding percentage of the deduction is lost.

    The phase-out range of $10,000 occurs from $61,000 to $71,000 (2015).

    The client has a modified AGI of $67,000, which encroaches into the phase-out range by $6,000. 

    • $6,000/$10,000 = 60% encroachment into the phase-out range 

    • Therefore, 60% of the deductibility is lost. 

    • Consequently, only 40% of the $2,000 contribution is deductible.

  • With modified AGI of $85,000, the $2,000 contribution would not be deductible at all.

Correct. The phase-out for this situation takes place when modified AGI is between $98,000 and $118,000 (2015). Since their modified AGI is $88,000, her contributions are fully deductible.
