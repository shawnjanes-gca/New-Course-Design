What is a Covered Recommendation?

A covered recommendation is any communication in which a covered advisor (1) earns compensation as a result of (2) providing investment advice to (3) a Retail Retirement Investor.1 A covered recommendation is made only when an advisor satisfies all three of these conditions.

Do You or Your Firm Receive Compensation?

The term “compensation” in this context includes direct compensation from the client and indirect compensation from third party sources. Neither variable compensation nor third party compensation is permitted unless an exemption applies.2

Variable compensation is simply compensation that varies based upon the type or amount of financial product sold: examples include up front commissions, trailing commissions, deferred sales charges, and 12(b)-1 fees.

The term “third party compensation” is any compensation from sources other than the client and includes revenue sharing, soft dollar arrangements, and other indirect payments.

Do You Provide Investment Advice?

The advisability of buying, selling, holding, or otherwise managing an investment (including ERISA plan and IRA rollovers) is investment advice. When you communicate that advice to a client you have made a recommendation. Even advice to change an existing investment relationship from brokerage (commission based) to advisory (flat fee or flat percentage based) may be considered a recommendation.

In contrast, communications of a general nature, for purely educational purposes, pursuant to a generalized allocation model, or as a platform provider will not generally rise to the level of a recommendation.

Example

A general newsletter to all clients discussing the current economic cycle and analyzing potential interest rate changes is generally not considered investment advice. Purely educational communications, such as methods to reduce capital gains taxes, would not generally be a recommendation.  However, if the communication includes specific investment advice, it may be viewed as a recommendation.

Is Your Client a Retail Retirement Investor?

The Rule is designed to protect individual (Retail) Retirement Investors rather than sophisticated institutional retirement investors. Using this principle as guidance, DOL identifies “[ERISA] plan participants and beneficiaries3 …[and] IRA owners as [Retail] Retirement Investors.” Notably, owners and beneficiaries of taxable accounts are not considered Retail Retirement Investors.

Here is one big change – participants in self-directed 401(k) plans (including those using a brokerage window) are now Retail Retirement Investors which, in turn, may lead to Fiduciary Advisor status. Before The Rule, advisors to these very same participants may have been exempted from fiduciary status.

Key Information: Effective Dates

The Rule is generally applicable as of April 10, 2017, but certain procedural and documentation requirements are delayed until January 1, 2018.
