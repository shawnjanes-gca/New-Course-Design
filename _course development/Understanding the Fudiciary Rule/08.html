Full Best Interest Contract Exemption (BICE)

Purpose

The Full BICE allows the payment of variable compensation to a Fiduciary Advisor, financial institution, and/or affiliate of the financial institution arising from recommendations to Retail Retirement Investors. Without the authority of the Full BICE, variable compensation would be a prohibited transaction subject to penalties.

Is the Full BICE a panacea for continuing business as usual during the “good old days” before The Rule?  Hardly! The Full BICE comes with sobering financial risk to both the advisor and the financial institution. As but one example of risk, IRA clients now have the right to file class action lawsuits against advisors and financial institutions for charging fees in excess of reasonable fees.  

The Full BICE is designed for ongoing relationships and serves to formalize the DOL’s imperative to make advice client-centric instead of advisor-centric.

You will see references to both “BICE” and “BIC” in the discussion that follows. “BICE” refers to a regulatory exemption1 created by the DOL as part of The Rule. A BIC is merely a document containing the provisions required by the BICE. It is a written, signed2, and legally binding contract between a financial institution and Retail Retirement Investor. A BIC must contain the following provisions:

FIDUCIARY ACKNOWLEDGEMENT

Advisor and financial institution must acknowledge they are acting as fiduciaries.

IMPARTIAL CONDUCT STANDARDS

Advisor and firm must agree to comply with the “Impartial Conduct Standards” summarized below.

<
>
  • Best Interest
  • Reasonable Compensation
  • No Misleading Statements

Recommendations must be based upon what is best for the client, not what makes the most money for the Fiduciary Advisor or financial institution.

The intimidating term “best” in this context is not absolute. An advisor need not evaluate every financial product from every financial product provider. Prudence3, not perfection, is required. By implication, a prudent recommendation requires a thorough knowledge of the client’s goals and financial profile. Thus, an element of financial planning may be required.

Excessive compensation is the DOL’s target with the reasonable compensation requirement. The Rule does NOT set advisor fees, but rather establishes principles-based guidelines within which fees must fall. As but one example, variable compensation paid to Fiduciary Advisors should be based upon “neutral factors” such as length of the sales cycle or expertise required to sell and service the investment.

The DOL admonishes Fiduciary Advisors to provide services commensurate with fees; the long-term result may be an evolution to service-based fee models and away from commission-based fee models.

Example – Services Commensurate with Fees

Fees may take into account advisor experience, credentials, and scope of services provided.  Consider these two advisors:

  • A CFP® professional with 30 years of experience meets with her client monthly and provides comprehensive financial planning services in addition to investment management services.
  • A non-credentialed advisor with 3 years of experience meets with his client annually and provides only investment management services.

Between these two advisors, the CFP® professional would generally be justified in charging a higher fee.  

An example of service-based pricing can be found in the hotel industry. The Ritz-Carlton provides premium service at premium prices to a discriminating clientele, while Super 8 caters to budget-conscious travelers seeking a basic room at modest cost.

How do we determine if fees are “reasonable?” A comparison of advisor fees, experience, credentials and services provided to fees for similar services provided by other advisors may be used to establish reasonable fees. This process is referred to as “benchmarking.”

Indexed Annuities were once advertised as “no fee” products. If a Fiduciary Advisor is paid commissions on the sale of such annuities, the term “no fee” may be deemed misleading under The Rule.

POLICIES AND PROCEDURES

Financial Institutions must design policies and procedures to enforce the best interest standard, most notably the impartial conduct standards. The DOL has authority to review the policies and procedures and, if found insufficient, revoke the firm’s BICE. Fines for engaging in prohibited transactions could then result.

ADVISOR INCENTIVES

Generations of incentive culture will change and change dramatically as the following common incentives are now forbidden:

  • Sales quotas
  • Sales based bonuses
  • Sales contests
  • Other incentives that could encourage advisors to act against the best interests of the client

FULL DISCLOSURE

Disclosure of conflicts of interest, including variable compensation such as commissions, was not required of many advisors before The Rule. Only Registered Investment Advisors and Fiduciaries to ERISA plans were required to disclose conflicts of interest.

The disclosure landscape changes powerfully at the applicability date of The Rule. Fiduciary Advisors and financial institutions must disclose all direct and third party compensation, including upfront commissions, deferred commissions, 12(b)1 fees, revenue sharing, and soft dollars.

One conflict of interest merits special consideration. Recommendations to purchase proprietary products represent conflicted advice and, while permitted under the Full BICE, require additional disclosure.

How will regulators track compliance with these BICE requirements? Compliance will be tracked in the same way as all other government requirements, i.e., documentation and lots of it! Warranties, disclosures, DOL notices, recordkeeping and other compliance requirements must be documented thoroughly.

No discussion of the BICE would be complete without a review of the “Thou shalt nots.” A BICE may NOT be used in the following circumstances:

  • The Fiduciary Advisor has discretionary control over ERISA plan or IRA rollovers or distributions.
  • Offering conflicted advice through a “Robo” advisor4
  • Conducting transactions between the Fiduciary Advisor personally and an ERISA plan or IRA
  • Attempting to limit the rights of the IRA Retail Retirement Investor5 to participate in a class action lawsuit, limit the amount that can be recovered under the suit, or place burdensome restrictions on the right to arbitration

1Technically referred to as “Prohibited Transaction Exemption 2016-01” by the DOL.

2A signed BIC is required from IRA clients only. A signed BIC is not required of ERISA plan clients but written disclosure of fiduciary status and adoption of policies designed to comply with the impartial conduct standards are required; these disclosures are referred to informally as the Disclosure BIC.

3 Prudence under The Rule is defined almost verbatim as prudence under the original 1974 ERISA statutes.

4A Robo advisor that steers clients to high commission and/or proprietary products may not be eligible for the BICE.

5These provisions will generally apply predominantly to IRA Retail Retirement Investors. ERISA requires an even higher standard for ERISA plan participants.
