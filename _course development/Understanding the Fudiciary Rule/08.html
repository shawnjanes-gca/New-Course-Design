Full Best Interest Contract Exemption (BICE)

Purpose

The Full BICE allows variable compensation to a Fiduciary Advisor, financial institution, and/or affiliate of the financial institution arising from recommendations to Retail Retirement Investors. Without the authority of the Full BICE, variable compensation would be a prohibited transaction subject to penalties.

Is the Full BICE a panacea for continuing business as usual during the “good old days” before The Rule?  Hardly! The Full BICE comes with sobering financial risk to both the advisor and the financial institution. As but one example of risk, IRA clients now have the right to file class action lawsuits against advisors and financial institutions for charging fees in excess of reasonable fees.  

The Full BICE is designed for ongoing relationships and serves to formalize the DOL’s imperative to make advice client-centric instead of advisor-centric.

You will see references to both “BICE” and “BIC” in the discussion that follows. “BICE” refers to a regulatory exemption1 created by the DOL as part of The Rule. How will regulators track compliance with these BICE requirements? Compliance will be tracked in the same way as all other government requirements, i.e., documentation and lots of it! Warranties, disclosures, DOL notices, recordkeeping and other compliance requirements must be documented thoroughly.

No discussion of the BICE would be complete without a review of the “Thou shalt nots.” A BICE may NOT be used in the following circumstances:

  • The Fiduciary Advisor has discretionary control over ERISA plan or IRA assets.
  • Offering conflicted advice through a “Robo” advisor 3
  • Conducting transactions between the Fiduciary Advisor personally and an ERISA plan or IRA
Attempting to limit the rights of the IRA Retail Retirement Investor4 to participate in a class action lawsuit, limit the amount that can be recovered under the suit, or place burdensome restrictions on the right to arbitration
