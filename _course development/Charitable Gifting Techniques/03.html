Charitable Income Tax Deductions

To obtain a charitable deduction on the Federal Estate, Gift, or Income Tax, a charity must be in the Treasury list of approved tax-exempt charities. This list is available in IRS Publication 78.

Do not confuse non-profit or not-for-profit organizations with tax-exempt organizations. The terms "non-profit" and "not-for-profit" are state law concepts and have nothing to do with the federal tax-exempt status of an organization. An organization must meet specific requirements of the Internal Revenue Code to qualify for tax-exempt status.

There is a limit to how much of a donor's adjusted gross income can be deducted for charitable gifts in a given year. Amounts that exceed that limit can generally be carried forward for five years. This limit is a function of the types of charities involved and the types of property being given.

Types of Charities

Charities can be classified into three types, with public charities receiving the most generous tax treatment.

Public Charities

Public charities are often referred to as 50% limit organizations because donations to them are generally limited to 50% of the donor's adjusted gross income (excluding any net operating loss carry back).

They include:

  • Churches

  • Synagogues

  • Colleges

  • Universities

  • Hospitals

  • U.S. Government

  • State Government

  • Private Operating Foundations

  • Private Distributing Foundations

  • Private Foundations that Maintain a Common Fund

  • Publicly supported corporations, trusts, or community chests, funds, or organizations that exist for charitable, religious, educational, scientific, or literary purposes, or to prevent cruelty to children or animals, or to foster certain amateur sports.

Semi-Public Charities

  • Veterans Organizations

  • Fraternal Societies

  • Nonprofit Cemeteries

Private Charities

Private Non-operating Foundations that do not meet the distribution requirements to be treated as a public charity.

 

 

Private Distributing Foundations are "nonoperating foundations," meaning that they themselves do not provide charitable services. However, they are considered public charities because they distribute 100% of contributions within 2 months following the year they receive the contributions.

Private Foundations that Maintain a Common Fund. These are private foundations that are operated for the benefit of public charities. The funds of contributors are pooled together, with all income distributed to public charities within 2 months following the tax year in which it was realized. They must also distribute the principal not later than 1 year after the donor's death or the death of the surviving spouse, if the spouse can name the recipient.

Private Operating Foundations use most of their income to provide charitable services or to run their own charitable programs. They make little in the way of charitable grants.
