Prohibition on Jeopardy Investments

To prevent private foundations from jeopardizing their principal through excessively risky investments, so-called "jeopardizing investments" are prohibited. While the law does not provide a "bright line" test, this rule is not especially stringent and will usually not constrain any reasonably prudent, diversified investment approach.

For example, even fairly unconventional investments such as futures, options, warrants, hedge funds, and the like may be allowed, but can be subjected to special scrutiny by the IRS1. If the client's foundation desires to invest in such assets, it would be advisable to consult an expert in advance and have adequate documentation prepared.

A related issue is margin trading. While not strictly prohibited, it is highly inadvisable in a private foundation. While the IRS does not strictly prohibit such margin trading in a foundation account, founders/directors could be personally responsible for any losses incurred while trading on margin. In other words, the risk/reward to such investing in a foundation account is lopsided against the founders/directors (if they make money, the foundation will get the benefit; if they lose money, they may be personally liable to the foundation for the loss). Even if the loss has nothing to do with the margining in the account, the founders/directors put themselves at risk for any loss. Thus, the prudent course is to avoid margin trading.
